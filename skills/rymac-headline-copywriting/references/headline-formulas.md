## 1. Awareness-stage diagnosis (Schwartz)

*Breakthrough Advertising*, 1966. Rule: "The more aware your market, the easier the selling job, the less you need to say." Diagnose stage BEFORE choosing a formula — this is the selector for everything below.

| Stage | Prospect knows | Headline leads with | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1. Unaware | Nothing | Problem recognition / symptoms; educate | "10 Signs Your Email List Is Dying" |
| 2. Problem aware | Feels pain, no fix category | Validate the frustration, name the root cause | "Why Your Leads Don't Answer" |
| 3. Solution aware | Solutions exist, comparing | Benefit/result + your differentiated mechanism | "Own your CRM outright — no monthly rent" |
| 4. Product aware | Knows your product, hesitating | Objection handling, proof, offer strength | "Will switching take weeks? No — one afternoon." |
| 5. Most aware | Ready, needs a nudge | The deal itself: price, offer, friction removal | Pricing + "Buy it once" |

SaaS homepage traffic is mostly stage 3 (solution-aware). Cold-traffic landing pages skew stage 2 (problem-aware) — lead harder on pain.

## 2. Hormozi value equation

**Value = (Dream Outcome × Perceived Likelihood) ÷ (Time Delay × Effort & Sacrifice)** — *$100M Offers*.

Template: **"[Dream outcome] in [short time], without [effort/sacrifice]"** with proof nearby.

Map to the hero:
- Dream outcome → headline (vivid, numbers, the reader sees themselves in it)
- Perceived likelihood → proof stack beside the claim (data → testimonials → case studies → demo)
- Time delay → quick-win signal ("see it in the first hour")
- Effort & sacrifice → friction removal named explicitly ("Paste your text. Click. Done in 30 seconds.")

Weak: "Learn how to improve your marketing." Strong: "In 90 days, 147 qualified leads waiting in your inbox — without spending a dollar on ads."

When to use: offer-driven pages, solution/product-aware traffic, anywhere the outcome and speed are honestly quantifiable.

## 3. Problem–Agitate–Solve (PAS)

(1) Name the problem in the reader's words → (2) agitate: cost, feelings, twist the knife → (3) present the product as the solve.

Hero application: H1 names the pain or its negation; subhead agitates or bridges; CTA delivers the solve. Copyhackers' default drill for headline practice. Canonical: Basecamp 2017 — "Your business is growing, and you're swamped. The way you're working isn't working anymore."

When to use: problem-aware audiences — pain felt, no solution picked. This is the writing-room house method; the hero is PAS compressed to three elements.

## 4. Before–After–Bridge (BAB)

Before (painful current state) → After (the paradise) → Bridge (product = how they get there). Starts aspirational instead of negative; sells the transformation.

When to use: when the dream outcome motivates more than the pain; solution-aware readers; positive brand moments.

## 5. "Stop X" / enemy positioning

**"Stop [painful status quo]"** or explicit us-vs-them against a villain the buyer already hates — a tool, a tax, a category, an incumbent. Never a person, never the reader.

Mechanism: shifts pressure off the seller — it's not seller vs. customer, it's *the two of you vs. the enemy*. Creates in-group trust. Strong B2B SaaS messaging "names an enemy the buyer already hates, speaks to one specific buyer, and describes the world after they win" (Pitch Kitchen).

Classics: HEY "Email sucked for years. Not anymore — we fixed it." · Loom "Share a video, not a calendar invite." · Oatly "It's like milk, but made for humans."

When to use: crowded categories where the point of view IS the differentiation; challenger brands (enemy positioning is a challenger's weapon — incumbents drift to category language). This is Allodra's native register.

## 6. Copyhackers homepage formulas

Joanna Wiebe, copyhackers.com/2022/07/headline-formulas:

1. **Quantity** — "Numeral + noun + verb + object" — Dropbox "Join over 700 million registered users…" (use when scale is the proof)
2. **Timed** — "[Do something hard] in [period]" — Crazy Egg "Make your website better. Instantly." (kills the time objection)
3. **Desired outcome** — "[Outcome] by [how]" (when outcome + mechanism both matter)
4. **Helping** — "Helping ___ do ___" — Copyblogger (instant audience self-selection)
5. **Paradoxical** — "Like a ___. Unlike any ___." — Motion "Like a calendar. Unlike any calendar you've ever seen." (familiar category, twist)

## 7. Voice-of-customer mining

Mine reviews, support tickets, sales calls, reply emails for the exact sentence customers use about the pain/outcome; promote the stickiest sentence to the headline nearly verbatim.

Canonical case: rehab center control "Your addiction ends here" vs mined "If you think you need rehab, you do" → **+400% CTA clicks, +20% form submissions**. "You don't write the headline; you find it."

For OathDriven: the mined sources are cold-email replies, discovery-call language, and the hooks that already earned replies (growth tax, renting-vs-owning, the 3-year number).

## 8. Harry Dry

Three title approaches (marketingexamples.com/landing-page/guide):
1. Explain what you do, simply (pass the caveman test)
2. What you do + a hook (address the biggest objection or pain in the same line)
3. Own your niche with conviction

Three tests every line must pass:
1. **Can you visualise it?** ("1,000 songs in your pocket")
2. **Can you falsify it?** ("Delivered in 30 minutes or it's free")
3. **Can nobody else say this?** ("The Breakfast of Champions")

Technique — **bleed the tap**: write 20+ versions; the first dozen are predictable; gold shows up late.

## 9. Julian Shapiro

Header litmus test: "If visitors read only this text, will they know exactly what you sell?" Fully descriptive, never a slogan. "Nailing header copy has the highest impact on whether people continue scrolling."

Value-prop derivation: ask **"What bad alternative do people resort to without your product, and how is your product better?"** → turn the answer into an action statement. (Duolingo: textbooks are the bad alternative → short interactive lessons. Allodra: renting GHL/HubSpot is the bad alternative → own it outright.)

Header options: (a) specific benefit ("Groceries delivered in 1 hour…"), (b) value prop + bold specific claim, (c) preempt the #1 objection in the headline (Webflow: power of code, no code required).

## 10. StoryBrand clarity rules

- **Grunt test:** 3–5 seconds on the hero must answer: What do you offer? How does it make my life better? How do I buy it?
- **"If you confuse, you lose."** Clarity creates sales; cute causes confusion.
- **Customer is the hero, brand is the guide** (Yoda, not Luke). Headline = their transformation, not your greatness.

## 11. MAGIC offer naming

Hormozi. For naming the offer itself (which often IS the headline on lead-gen/program pages): **M**agnet (Free/Sale) + **A**vatar (who) + **G**oal (outcome) + **I**nterval (timeframe) + **C**ontainer (Challenge/Blueprint/Accelerator). Use 3–5 of the 5. "Free 12-Week Beach Body Challenge for Busy Moms."

## 12. The 20 headline rules

1. Clarity beats cleverness — confusion is the #1 conversion killer. (StoryBrand, Copyhackers)
2. Be fully descriptive of what you sell. (Julian)
3. Specificity beats generality — "Groceries in 1 hour" > "Improve your workflow." (Julian)
4. Make it visualizable — concrete images stick. (Dry)
5. Make it falsifiable — precise claims get believed. (Dry)
6. Make it un-copyable — else it's a category label. (Dry)
7. One big idea per headline. (Rule of One)
8. Match the market's awareness stage. (Schwartz)
9. Lead with the dream outcome; raise likelihood, cut time, cut effort. (Hormozi)
10. Use the customer's own words — mined beats invented. (Wiebe, +400% case)
11. Pass the "so what" test — bridge every feature to a felt benefit with "which means that…".
12. Concrete numbers; odd/precise numbers read as counted, not invented (~36% CTR lift for numbers).
13. Keep it ~6–12 words above the fold.
14. Write at ~5th–7th grade reading level — comprehension friction is conversion friction.
15. Bold but credible — hyperbole suppresses perceived likelihood. (Hormozi)
16. Talk to one reader; route multiple personas, don't average them.
17. Customer is the hero; you're the guide. (StoryBrand)
18. Write 20+ variants before choosing. (Dry, Wiebe)
19. Optionally preempt the #1 objection in the headline itself. (Julian, Webflow)
20. A curiosity headline must be paid off — no unpayable checks. (Copyhackers)
