# Hero Section Anatomy & CTA Patterns

Synthesized from Julian Shapiro's Landing Page Handbook, Demand Curve's above-the-fold playbook, Harry Dry's landing-page guide, and CXL/Unbounce conversion research.

## Hero anatomy — element by element, top to bottom

Each element has exactly one job. If an element isn't doing its job, cut or fix it.

1. **Nav bar** — get out of the way. 2–4 links + one visually dominant CTA.
2. **Eyebrow / badge** (optional) — set context or credibility in ≤6 words (category, audience filter, or a definition — e.g. Allodra's "/ al·lo·di·al — owned, free and clear /").
3. **Headline (H1)** — carry the one big idea. Pass the grunt test in ~5 seconds: what you sell, for whom, why it matters. Highest-leverage element on the page.
4. **Subheadline** — make the headline believable and concrete. It answers "how?": introduces the product, names the mechanism, or kills the top objection. 1–2 sentences max. Rule of thumb: **headline = the value; subhead = how you create it.** (Harry Dry: "Get specific. Introduce the product.")
5. **CTA button** — the actionable next step to fulfilling the headline's claim. Reads as a continuation of the hero's story, never a generic verb.
6. **Click-trigger microcopy** beside/under the button — kill last-second risk: "No credit card required" (+19% signups documented), "Takes 30 seconds", "One-time — own it forever."
7. **Social proof strip** — make the claim believable at the moment it's made. Customer count, star rating, or client logos directly under the headline/CTA. Hero-placed proof ≈ 2.3× engagement vs the same proof below the fold; proof adjacent to the CTA transfers credibility onto the button.
8. **Hero visual** — let the reader visualize the value: real UI screenshot, short demo loop, or the outcome itself. Never abstract decoration that competes with the message.

## Below the fold (for when the task extends past the hero)

Harry Dry's sequence: features tied back to the hero's value prop as one running narrative → objections answered in customer language → story-form testimonials (specific problem + result + timeframe) → FAQ → repeated CTA → founder's note ("people buy from people").

Global filter: **"Would this help me sell if I met the customer in person? If not, remove it."**

## CTA patterns

1. **Continue the story.** If the hero says "Groceries in 1 hour," the button says "Find food," not "Submit." A CTA nobody earned ("Request a meeting") fails regardless of color or size. (Julian)
2. **Call to value, not call to action.** `[verb] + [the thing they actually want]`: "Get my free plan" / "See the 3-year math" / "Start closing more jobs" — never "Sign up" / "Submit" / "Learn more". (Dry)
3. **First person converts.** "Start **my** free 30-day trial" beat "your" by +90% CTR (Michael Aagaard / Unbounce); replications typically +10–40%. Default to first person, then test.
4. **Compress the value equation into the button:** action + outcome + optional time — "Get my score in 30 seconds."
5. **De-risk at the point of click.** Risk-killers on or beside the button: "No credit card required," "Cancel anytime," "Join 4,000+ shops." Answer the objection at the exact moment of decision.
6. **Put proof next to the button.** Trust transfers onto the click.
7. **Match CTA weight to awareness stage.** Most-aware → hard CTA ("Buy it once", "Start free trial"). Solution/product-aware with a complex product → soften the first CTA ("See how it works") and repeat the hard CTA lower with a fresh reason.
8. **One offer, multiple placements.** Repeat the CTA down the page; never introduce a second competing offer. (Rule of One)
