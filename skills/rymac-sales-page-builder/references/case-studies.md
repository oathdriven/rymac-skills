# Case studies: the same skeleton, three offers

Three live pages built on the identical skeleton. Read this to internalize that the **bones never change - only the PAS engine and the clothes do.** The strongest proof that the pattern is real: these three sell wildly different things (a $900 one-time CRM, a $100/mo veteran sales system, a $49/mo sales-practice app) to wildly different buyers, and the section order is the same.

## Allodra
**Offer:** own your CRM/AI stack outright (one-time ~$900) instead of renting a SaaS. **Buyer:** agency/operator paying the "growth tax." **Traffic:** two front doors.

- **Hero = a reframe, not a claim.** "Your CRM isn't Software. It's a *Landlord*." The villain (the digital landlord / rent / growth tax) is named in the headline. No price, no buy button - the CTA is *Apply To Qualify*, an application, not a checkout. Job: get the VSL watched, push to the analyzer.
- **PAS engine = the analyzer (a calculator).** The prospect indicts their *own* setup: they type their company name, pick "Who's your landlord?" from the top-6 CRMs (each preset loaded with real running costs), enter spend + client count. Headline agitates: *"What Is Renting Your CRM Really Costing You?"* It submits their numbers to a report that renders one giant red **10-Year True Cost** number - *their* math, framed as their own. Then, with no gate, it turns to the solution: *"Ready to stop building their equity and start building yours?"*
- **Two valid front doors (important for the hero-job call):** (A) warm: VSL hero -> analyzer. (B) cold: the analyzer *is* the hero (the standalone `allodial-analyzer` lander, cold-email merge-tag `?company=` auto-fills). Same PAS engine, different depth of run-up. **Lesson: on cold traffic you can promote the PAS engine to the hero.**
- **Pricing withheld until the FAQ** because the funnel wants the application first. The calculator *is* the pricing argument (one-time $900 vs $13k+/yr of rent).
- **A paper P.A.S. letter runs UNDER the calculator** (a second PAS beat, like Sierra-6's calculator-after-letter, in reverse). The full 23-beat RyMac letter (`rymac-direct-sales-copy`), rendered as a physical sheet popping off the dark page - letterhead, black ink, red-X pains, green-check outcomes, blue hyperlinks out to Sierra-6 + the EOFire interview. The pattern break makes the page feel like real mail from a real operator.
- **The FAQ is SEO/AEO-tuned:** questions retitled to verbatim search queries ("Is this a GoHighLevel alternative?", "Is this a one-time payment CRM with no monthly fees?"), answer-first sentences, FAQPage JSON-LD, and one varied-anchor "read more" link per answer into the LIVE blog (new tab). It handles objections AND earns search traffic.
- **Footer + legal shipped with the page:** a Disclaimer page backs the "free infrastructure" and competitor claims (free tiers real but not guaranteed zero-cost; not affiliated with Google/Vercel/GHL/etc.); the footer opens every outbound link in a new tab so the sales page never leaks.

## Sierra-6
**Offer:** an AI sales system for veteran home-service shops ($100/mo for veterans). **Buyer:** the burned-out veteran owner losing jobs to slow follow-up. **Traffic:** cold + referral.

- **Qualifier bar first:** "This Page Is Only For Veteran-Owned Home-Service Business Owners." Disqualifying is a conversion device - it makes the right reader lean in.
- **Hero = promise + mechanism, earns the scroll.** "Book 4x More Estimates in 48 Hours" using "The 3 Laws of Landed Jobs," with three negation bullets (without buying leads / hiring an agency / duct-taping tools). CTA is a scroll-anchor to `#book`, not a form. Risk-reversal ticks under it.
- **PAS engine = a long P.A.S. sales letter** ("Dear Fellow Veteran," signed "Always got your back, RyMac"). Its structure IS the template:
  1. **Pain checklist** ("let me know if this sounds like you") - ghosted leads, $100 leads that curse you out, agencies that took thousands.
  2. **Dream-outcome contrast** - wake up to booked estimates, time to fish.
  3. **Agitate + name the villain** - the shared HomeAdvisor lead (*"You're the 5th call today!"*), and the villain cameo: *"some kid from ABC Agency... wants your credit card so he can make a commission."* Capped with *"It's like Groundhog Day, ain't it?"*
  4. **Founder origin story with a quantified win** - Tom the tree guy bet his last $1,500, 90 days later: *"I LANDED A $50,000 CONTRACT."*
  5. **Turn to solution** - the "3 Laws," a green-check feature list, a free website, "results in 48 hours."
  6. **Future-pace + P.S. close** - *"Let me cook, and you eat."*
- **Then a calculator** quantifies the speed-to-lead loss in dollars (a second PAS beat).
- **Value stack -> anchor-then-reveal:** offer stack items priced $100-$1,000/mo each, total "$7,500 + $2,500/mo" struck against **$100/mo** for the next 10 veterans, wrapped in the bold **"You and Me Guarantee"** (if my first 30 days doesn't beat your last 30, I pay you $250 + donate $250).

## closectl
**Offer:** an AI-prospect sales-practice app ($49/mo). **Buyer:** builders/reps terrified to pick up the phone. **Traffic:** cold/paid.

- **Hero earns the click.** "Practice the sales call you keep avoiding." Villain = the freeze. Sub = the mechanism ("an AI prospect calls your real phone... without burning a single lead").
- **PAS engine = a free experience of the product.** They pick a prospect and one calls their real phone. They *feel* the fear and the fumble - which makes them realize they need the board and the framework. The page then hands both over free (the live call board, the framework, the objections). **This is the newest form of the PAS engine: instead of telling them the pain (letter) or calculating it (calculator), you let them live it.**
- **Give-it-away mechanism section.** The board + framework + objections are the free product, in-page, no signup - still capturing the lead. Each free thing is a micro-yes toward the buy.
- **Value stack** = 7 rows (piece + $ value + pain removed) totaling $5,282, "priceless" row for the confidence, framing the $49.
- **Proof** = the David transcript, the receipts, and the founder story ("I made up a company on the spot and closed him for four grand... I had nothing, and it closed. You have a real product.").
- **Signatures reused:** "What's on your mind?" FAQ, "page can't scroll any further" final CTA.

## Constant vs changes

**Always constant:**
- The skeleton and its order (hero -> PAS -> mechanism -> value -> proof -> pricing -> FAQ -> final CTA -> footer).
- A PAS engine sits directly after the hero. Non-negotiable.
- A footer that carries the legal pages (Terms/Privacy/Disclaimer), parent-company copyright, and real socials, with every outbound link opening a new tab. Ship a Disclaimer whenever the page makes cost or competitor claims.
- Anchor-then-reveal pricing (show the big value/number, then the small price).
- A guarantee or stacked risk-reversal near the price.
- FAQ dead-ends into the CTA. Warm, objection-by-objection.
- One villain named in the hero and echoed down the page.
- Founder story with at least one quantified, falsifiable win.
- "Featured on Entrepreneurs on Fire" as recurring third-party authority.
- The signature FAQ header and final-CTA line.

**Always changes (design these per offer):**
- **The PAS engine's FORM** - letter (emotional/story-driven buyer), calculator (numbers/ROI buyer), or free demo (experiential product). Pick by what makes the pain most undeniable for *this* buyer.
- Whether the hero converts alone or earns the scroll (traffic temperature).
- Whether pricing shows on-page or is withheld for an application/call.
- The recapture layer (exit-pop, chat widget, or none).
