## Value equation
Every element should raise the top or cut the bottom of:
```
            dream outcome  x  perceived likelihood of success
value  =  ------------------------------------------------------
              time delay   x   effort and sacrifice
```
- Raise **dream outcome**: paint the specific, visualizable after-state.
- Raise **likelihood**: proof beside every claim (receipts, the founder win, real numbers).
- Cut **time**: "in 48 hours," "10 minutes," "in about a minute."
- Cut **effort/sacrifice**: "without X," "no card," "nothing to install," "we did the recon for you."
A page that only agitates pain floats away; a page that only promises outcome is not believed. Do both, everywhere.

## One villain
- **One villain, named in the hero and echoed down the page.** A tool, a practice, an incumbent, or a feeling - never the reader's own failure. "the growth tax" / "the ABC-agency kid" / "the freeze." If the villain fades after the hero, the page loses its spine. Touch it in the PAS engine, the value stack, and the final CTA.
- **One promise, carried the whole way.** The hero's promise is the pricing justification and the final-CTA payoff. Do not switch promises mid-page.
- **One refrain** - the 5-word version of the promise ("until the calls stop being scary," "take title," "let me cook, you eat"). Seed it near the top and close on it. Bookending makes it stick.
- **Never shame the reader.** Name the villain and the pain, but frame the escape, never force a confession. "Practice without the judgment" sells; "the call you keep avoiding" makes them admit they are avoiding, and people do not buy from a page that just made them feel small. The reader is the hero; the villain is the tool/practice/feeling.

## Readability
Write at a **5th-to-8th grade reading level**. Short sentences. Plain words over clever ones. A confused reader does not buy, and clever costs comprehension. Sweep every draft through the free Hemingway app (hemingwayapp.com) and cut until it reads clean. This applies to the whole page, headline to FAQ.

## Micro-yes
Momentum is built from small commitments. Each free thing (a calculator run, a report, a free call, a given-away framework) is a "yes" that makes the buy the next natural yes. Structure the page so the reader keeps agreeing before they are ever asked to pay. Giving away real value is not a leak; it is the ladder.

## Specificity
- Odd, exact numbers read as counted, not invented: "71% on $30,000 deals," "10 minutes, 4 seconds," "13 checkpoints," "$5,190 commission."
- **Falsifiable claims get believed; vague ones get ignored.** "Beats your last 30 days or I pay you $250" beats "amazing results."
- Concrete detail is proof: "he paid with a Visa that starts with a 4, because Visa cards start with 4."

## Risk reversal
Put a risk-killer beside every ask, not just the final one: "Free, no card," "cancel anytime," "founding price locked for life," "one close pays for the year," and a real guarantee box near the price (Sierra-6's "You and Me Guarantee" is the model - the founder takes the downside). The bigger the ask, the louder the reversal.

## Anchor then reveal
Always show the big number before the small one. Stack the value (or strike a higher price) so the actual price feels tiny by contrast: "$7,500 + $2,500/mo... $100/mo for the next 10 veterans," "$5,282 of value... $49 a month," "$13k/yr of rent... $900 one time." Never lead with the price naked.

## Awareness
The reader's awareness stage sets the hero's job (see SKILL.md table):
- **Most-aware / warm** (retargeting, referral, they know you): hero can close; CTA is the buy.
- **Problem-aware / cold** (paid ads, first touch): hero earns the scroll; lead harder on the pain; the PAS engine does the converting. On very cold traffic you can promote the PAS engine to the hero itself (Allodra's analyzer-as-hero front door).

## CTA patterns
- **Value-based, first person.** "Have Jamie call me" beats "Submit." "Show me the plans" beats "Pricing." "See the 3-year math" beats "Learn more." First person ("my," "me") reliably out-converts second person.
- **Match CTA weight to awareness.** Cold: a soft first CTA (see how it works / calculate my cost / practice free). Warm: the hard CTA (buy it, book the call).
- **One decision per screen.** Do not offer two competing CTAs in the same view; it splits the yes.
- **The two-path close** at the end: name the bad path (keep suffering the villain) against the good path (take the step). It makes the click feel like the obvious escape.

## Honesty
Only claims you will defend publicly. One inflated claim next to your best proof poisons the credibility of both - the reader cannot tell which numbers are real, so they discount all of them. Protect your falsifiable anchor (the 71%, the receipts) by surrounding it only with claims that are equally true. The honesty IS the edge; never trade it for a bigger number.
