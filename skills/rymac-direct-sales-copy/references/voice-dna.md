## 1. Formatting fingerprint

Three treatments, three jobs. Never mix them up:

| Treatment | Job | Examples from the specimen |
|---|---|---|
| **Bold** (`<strong>`) | Claims, pains, promises — the skim path | "Thousands of Veterans struggle with the same issues every day." · "It's aggravating as fire. I get it." · "I'll build it FREE." · "as fast as 48 hours." |
| <u>Underline</u> (`<u>`) | Dream outcomes and identity lines (rare, ~6 per letter) | "You want to wake up to booked estimates." · "you're good at what you do." · "I wanted to help people." |
| *Italics* (`<em>`) | Quoted speech, sneered enemy words, felt-identity wounds | *"high-intent,"* · "You feel more like a professional voicemail-leaver than a business owner." · watching sub-par contractors land *your* jobs |

**The skim test (mandatory):** reading ONLY the bold lines top to bottom must read as a compressed version of the whole letter — pain → promise → proof → speed → imagine. If the bold path doesn't tell the story alone, re-mark the letter.

Mechanics:
- **One-sentence paragraphs dominate.** Hard cap: 3 sentences per paragraph. "Letters, not essays."
- **Bullets appear exactly twice**: the "sounds like you" pain questions going in, the system outcomes coming out. No other lists.
- **Pull-quotes**: spoken lines get their own set-off paragraph. Two per letter — one enemy voice (the cursing lead), one client voice (the triumph call, which can go ALL CAPS + brand color).
- **Scare quotes constantly** on the enemy's vocabulary: "ghosted," "no-shows," "tire-kickers," "other guys," "demo," "high-intent." Signals: that's THEIR word, and we both know it's garbage.
- **Ellipses as cliffhangers** handing off to the next paragraph: "But…" · "And since that day in 2011…"
- **Underline can nest inside bold** for single-word stress: "The 3 Laws to <u>Landed</u> Jobs."
- **No em dashes** in customer-facing copy (hard rule from the voice docs — use a comma, period, colon, or parentheses). **No orphaned words** (a lone word wrapping to its own line).

## 2. Diction and cadence

- **Contractions always**: it's, you're, I've, don't, ain't. Tag questions as a verbal tic: "It's like Groundhog Day, ain't it?" (His sales law: "Always end in a question. Never end in a sentence.")
- **Southern/blue-collar idiom**: "aggravating as fire", "half-stepping", "the whole nine", "damn good", "Hell, you just want…", "your current website sucks".
- **Marine/veteran lexicon worn casually, never as a medal case**: boot camp, Parris Island, platoon, buddy program, "Always got your back." Military detail is texture, not a flex.
- **Specific numbers over adjectives**: $100 lead, $1,500, 90 days, $50,000, 2011, 15 years, 5 minutes, 48 hours, 15-minute chat. Odd/precise numbers read as counted, not invented.
- **Named real people**: Tom the tree-removal guy, "some kid from ABC Agency." Story people always get names, trades, dollar figures, and years.
- **Second-person density**: "you/your" in nearly every sentence of the first half.
- **Anaphora runs**: "You want…" ×5 in the empathy run; "Imagine…" ×3 in the future-pace.
- **3-beat staccato for loops**: "Wake up. Rinse. Repeat."
- **Everyman references**: Groundhog Day, Applebee's, darts, pool, fishing. Pop culture a 45-year-old tradesman actually knows.
- **Sentence length**: mostly 5–15 words. 5th–8th grade reading level (Hemingway-app clean).
- **Signoff is LOCKED**: "Always got your back,<br>RyMac"

## 3. Persuasion habits

- **Pain fully paid before pitch.** ~40% of the letter is empathy + agitation before the writer's name appears. The pivot line is agreement, not solution: "It's aggravating as fire. I get it."
- **Disqualify before qualify.** Say what you are NOT first ("I'm not an agency, and I don't sell leads") — inoculation before the intro.
- **Enemy is always a specific middleman** profiting off the reader's work: HomeAdvisor, Angi, "some kid from ABC Agency" who "wants your credit card so he can make a commission," shared-lead mills, the landlord/growth tax. **Never the reader's own failure.**
- **Proof via story, not stats.** One named client arc carries the credibility load (desperation → the bet → the timeframe → the dollar quote → the aftermath: "multiple locations, trucks, the whole nine"). Borrowed authority used once, humbly framed: "Entrepreneur on Fire tracking me down… I had no idea I'd done anything 'special.'"
- **Price anchored to everyday objects**: "for less than what your family dinner at Applebee's costs."
- **Future-pacing negates the agitation one-for-one.** Each "Imagine" line un-does a specific scene painted earlier. Don't invent new dreams at the close; pay off the pains already named.
- **Soft-qualifier CTA, never a hard close**: "Let's have a 15-minute chat and see if it makes sense." Diagnosis frame (business doctor), takeaway energy.
- **Humor as disarm, mid-tension**, then take the mic back: the Boston buddy-program aside → "But…"
- **Food/cooking metaphor for done-for-you** (signature P.S. move): "Let me cook, and you eat."
- **Radical honesty IS the pitch**: give away real value, never overpromise, "the trust is the moat."
- **Risk reversal stacked at every CTA**: Free Website · No Contracts · No Set-Up Fees (or the offer's equivalents).

## 4. The never list

- No hype-bro tone: no "crush it," "10X," "insane results," exclamation stacking. Confident, not hypey.
- No fake scarcity. Only real caps ("we take X builds a month").
- No corporate speak: "in today's world," "unlock synergies," "leverage," "solutions," "streamline."
- No paraphrasing verbatim signature lines — they are untouchable tokens (see voice-lines.md if writing for Ryan).
- No em dashes, no orphaned words, in customer-facing copy.
- No overpromising. The honesty is the edge; never trade it for a bigger number.
- No selling before pain agreement. The pitch never appears before "I get it."
- No feature weeds. Bullets are outcomes ("wake up to booked sales calls"), never tech specs. "Weeds don't close deals."
- No essay paragraphs (3-sentence hard cap).
- No anonymous proof. Never "a client of mine" — the client has a name, a trade, a dollar figure, and a year.
- No arguing with the reader. "Nobody buys from someone they're arguing with."
- No heavy emoji (a single badge-level emoji max, and only in email subjects/badges — not letters).

## 5. Writing as someone else

This skill is shared. When the letter's signer is NOT Ryan, keep every structural and formatting rule above but swap the personal tokens. Run this intake as a short interview, one question at a time, showing Ryan's answer as the example for each. Save the answers to `${CLAUDE_SKILL_DIR}/my-voice-profile.md` under these same 7 headings so the user answers once, ever (see First-run setup in SKILL.md):

1. **Identity bond** — what do you and the reader have in common? (Ryan's: "Dear Fellow Veteran")
2. **What you are NOT** — the two disqualifiers your industry's enemy IS. (Ryan's: "not an agency, don't sell leads")
3. **Origin story** — first client's name, trade, what they risked, the timeframe, the dollar result, the quote they said to you.
4. **Credibility marker** — one borrowed-authority moment, humbly framed. (Ryan's: EOFire interview)
5. **Idiom** — 3–5 phrases you actually say. (Ryan's: "aggravating as fire," "the whole nine")
6. **Signoff** — your locked signature line. (Ryan's: "Always got your back, RyMac")
7. **Price anchor** — the everyday purchase your price undercuts. (Ryan's: Applebee's family dinner)

Never ship Ryan's personal tokens (Parris Island, Tom, EOFire, "Always got your back") under someone else's name.
