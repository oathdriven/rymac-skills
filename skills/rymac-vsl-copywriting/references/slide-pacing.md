## 1. Reading speed & words per slide

- Silent reading: ~238–250 wpm ≈ **4 words/sec**. Reading along with VO is bound by voice speed: conversational VO = **140–160 wpm ≈ 2.3–2.7 words/sec**. Eyes finish ~1.5–2× ahead of the voice — that beat of "waiting for the voice to confirm" is the lock-in effect.
- Subtitle comprehension ceiling: **17 characters/second** comfortable; above ~20 cps rereading stops and comprehension drops (eye-tracking studies).
- **Words per slide: 5–15, one idea.** Hooks 5–8 words. Sentences past ~15 words split across slides. (House Sierra-6 slides run 5–28 words, avg ~16 — the long ones carry a two-line setup/punch break; don't exceed that.)
- **On-screen text is verbatim VO** — or an exact fragment of it. Never paraphrase: eye/ear mismatch breaks the trance (and tests worse, even though it "feels" redundant).

## 2. Slide duration

- **Formula: DUR = word count ÷ 2.5 + hold.** 8 words ≈ 3.2s; 12 words ≈ 4.8s.
- **Range 2–5s, cap 6s.** Floor **1s** (even a single-word punch holds ≥1s). Only scripted dramatic holds pass 6s, never past ~8s of static screen.
- **Slides per minute: ~12–15** at 150 wpm and ~10 words/slide. Budget: 4-min VSL ≈ 560–640 words ≈ 45–60 slides.
- Sanity totals on every script: Σ DUR = runtime · total words ÷ runtime = 140–160 wpm.

## 3. Chunking rules

1. **One idea per slide.** Write the script AS slides from the start (Benson: "you're writing sentence fragments"), never prose chopped later.
2. **Split past ~15 words** at the natural clause break — setup clause on slide A, payoff clause on slide B. The break goes where you want the listener to feel the beat.
3. **The cut is the punctuation.** Rapid short slides = staccato urgency; one held slide = gravity.
4. **Forward-motion endings:** mid-thought slides end with **"..." or ":"** so the eye is pulled to the next slide. Use for open loops: "And that's when I found out..." → cut → the reveal.
5. **Single-word / single-number slides** for maximum emphasis: the price, "Nothing.", "Why?" — alone on the slide, held longer than reading requires. Isolation is emphasis.
6. **Cliffhanger slides:** a question or half-claim as its own slide, hold, answer on the next. The break manufactures curiosity mechanically.
7. **Two sentences = two slides.** Exception: a setup line + punch line stacked as two paragraph lines on one slide (the house Sierra-6 move) — the punch line carries the highlight.
8. **Lists fire as slide runs:** one item per slide at 2–3s each. Three fast slides in a row read as momentum — use for value stacks and pain recaps.

## 4. Pause & hold playbook

Silence only registers because the baseline is fast. Budget ~1 long hold per 60–90s of runtime; if everything holds, nothing holds.

| Hold | Length | Use on |
|---|---|---|
| [BEAT] | 0.5–1s | Between chunked clauses; before a reveal word (pre-pause improves recall of the word that follows) |
| [HOLD 2-3s] | 2–3s | The workhorse. After big claims, questions the viewer should mentally answer, key stats. 3s is the sweet spot |
| [HOLD 4-5s] | 4–5s | Dramatic, rare — 1–3 per VSL. The price alone on screen. The gut-punch story line. The twist |

- Pause BEFORE the reveal word, hold AFTER the reveal lands.
- The price/number sits alone on its slide in silence 2–3s after the VO says it.
- Never more than 5s of dead air.
- **Script every hold explicitly** — [HOLD 2s], [BEAT] in the NOTES column. Editors won't invent pauses.

## 5. On-screen readability

- 1–3 short centered lines per slide; break lines at phrase boundaries; never orphan a word. (Subtitle norms: 37–42 chars/line, 2 lines max — big-type slides run shorter.)
- **Sentence case for body lines.** All-caps flattens word shapes and slows reading — reserve for a 1–3 word punch slide or one emphasized word.
- **Highlight exactly 1–2 words per slide** (house: red #C8102E on the emotional/numeric payload). More than 2 = no emphasis.
- Mobile floor: ≥36px at 1080×1920; primary text 48–72px; bold sans-serif; contrast ≥4.5:1.
- **Keep animation dumb:** cut, fade, or scale-in. Per-letter fly-ins slow comprehension and underperform. The words are the visual.
- **Consistency is the trance:** same font, size, position every slide. Layout changes are spent deliberately as pattern interrupts, never as decoration. (Visual spec lives in the design doc — the script only flags deviations in NOTES.)

## 6. Retention pacing across the video

- Something changes on screen every **2–4 seconds** — the slide cut natively hits high-retention cadence (top short-form averages one cut per 2–4s; ~2.5s avg clip correlated with +35% completion vs 4s+).
- **Hook (first 15s): fastest.** 1.5–3s slides, 5–8 words, loop opened immediately.
- **Story/agitation (middle): breathe.** 3–6s slides, the 2–3s emotional holds live here.
- **Close: re-accelerate.** Short declarative slides, value stack fired at 2–3s each, HOLD on price, fast CTA slides.
- Vary durations in triplets (2s–4s–3s) — constant length reads as monotone.
- One pattern interrupt every 20–30s: a single-word slide, a number slide, a question slide, a color inversion.

## 7. Why text-only converts

- **Moreno & Mayer 2002:** narration + identical on-screen text beats narration alone **when the text is the only visual** — no B-roll, no graphics competing for the eye. That is exactly this format. (Benson's folk version: "reading + listening = 68% more information retained.")
- **Forced linear consumption:** a sales page lets the prospect skim to the price; a slide VSL walks them through the entire argument at your pace. Long-run data: ugly text VSLs outperform the same copy as a text page by ~10%+, and routinely beat $15K productions.
- Viewers *prefer* identical text+narration (Yue/Bjork 2013) — and preference drives watch time in a sales context.
