## 1. The retention-timed 8-beat template

Best single template (greenfroglabs data). Scale timings proportionally to target runtime.

| Beat | 12-min timing | 5-min scaled | Retention entering | Beat-specific failure |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Hook | 0:00–0:30 | 0:00–0:30 | 100% | weak hook loses 30–50% in first 8s |
| Problem | 0:30–2:00 | 0:30–1:15 | 65–75% | wrong problem framing |
| Agitation | 2:00–3:30 | 1:15–2:00 | 55–65% | >90s feels manipulative |
| Solution/mechanism | 3:30–5:00 | 2:00–2:45 | 45–55% | generic solution meets agitated pain |
| Proof | 5:00–7:00 | 2:45–3:30 | 40–50% | proof feels staged |
| Offer | 7:00–9:00 | 3:30–4:15 | 35–45% | price before value stack |
| Objections | 9:00–11:00 | 4:15–4:45 | 28–35% | >5 objections = desperate |
| Close/CTA | 11:00–12:00 | 4:45–5:00 | 22–30% | vague or buried CTA |

Simpler 6-step consulting skeleton (Wealthy Consultant): **Market Callout → Pain → Desire → Offer → Risk Reversal → Clear CTA.** Their contrarian rule for sophisticated/warm audiences: "offer early, offer often" — an early offer filters; decisive buyers don't need 10 minutes of persuasion. Cold traffic still gets problem-first education.

## 2. Jon Benson — 3X VSL formula

Inventor of the text-slide VSL. The "critical 500 words" = pattern interrupt + lead; they make or break the video.

**Step 1 — Snap Suggestion (open, ~0:00–0:45):** Triple Pattern Interrupt (3 rapid intrigue statements, each tied to a benefit of watching — kills the "this is a sales video" signal) → "Now that you..." presuppositional anchors (assumes they're qualified and watching) → "Everybody knows..." (undeniable shared truth) → "However..." (the contrarian turn that opens the first loop).

**Step 2 — The Big Problem (~0:45–3:00):** Biggest problem, quick hit → 5-part case for it → "Not only that..." → "And another thing..." → "So that means..." (consequence stacking) → "Listen to me because" (credibility dropped MID-problem, never up front) → the ultimatum (change or this continues) → ultimate pain result (bottom of the pit).

**Step 3 — The Bigger Solution (~3:00–5:00):** Good-news hook → "There's a formula" (name the system — the mechanism gets a proper noun) → social proof → "a realistic shot" ("even if you've failed before") → close the first hook loop.

Then: offer → price justification → guarantee → CTA. Rules: USP inside the first 10 slides; "reluctant hero" story frame; write the script AS slide-sized sentence fragments from the start.

## 3. Sabri Suby — application-funnel skeleton

The King Kong model — VSL above a qualifying survey → calendar:
1. Call out the audience by name/segment
2. Big promise + plain explanation
3. Irresistible intrigue bullets (fears, pains, hopes)
4. Floodlight on the problem (they can't fix it alone)
5. "Listen to me because" credibility
6. Proof + social proof
7. The Godfather Offer (value-stacked, risk-reversed, too good to refuse)
8. Real scarcity / reason to act now
9. CTA → survey/application → booked call

The 97% not ready get value-first nurture videos (**Magic Lantern**) — each teaches, each ends with a soft "book a time" CTA. This is the template for the invisible-VSL YouTube ladder.

## 4. Alex Hormozi — book-a-call beats

**Niche callout → paint the pain → epiphany bridge story → credibility → Most Valuable Offer → proof → price anchor → concern handling → guarantee + scarcity → next steps (book the call, pre-frame what happens on it).**
Principles: start with pain, never intros; stack value until no-brainer (value = dream outcome × likelihood ÷ time × effort); for high-ticket the CTA is the call, so pre-frame the call itself; blunt CTA language; test multiple hooks.

## 5. Russell Brunson — Epiphany Bridge

The story/credibility beat, 5 steps: (1) life before — external problem + internal fear + failed attempts → (2) journey & conflict → (3) the "aha" / new opportunity (this IS the mechanism reveal in story form — a new vehicle, not an improvement on what failed) → (4) the framework — **teach what/why, never how** (reserving the how preserves the reason to book) → (5) successes — yours, then others'.

## 6. Hook patterns

Retention reality: the viewer decides in 5–8 seconds (Wistia, 1.2M videos); steepest drop seconds 10–20; the hook must pay off its promise by ~0:15. Three-phase open: **pattern interrupt (0–5s) → specific payoff promise (5–15s) → commitment hook (15–30s)**.

1. **Problem-first callout** — "If you spend $5,000/mo on ads and can't track which half works..." Beat claim-first in 9 of 12 cold-traffic A/B tests; one documented swap lifted conversion 4.2%→7.8%.
2. **Big claim + timeframe** — "This one system can double your booked estimates without spending a dollar on ads."
3. **Qualifying question** — "Are you a veteran-owned shop doing $500K+ but still answering your own phones?"
4. **Pattern interrupt** — unexpected statement/visual; Benson's triple version.
5. **Conditional stakes** — "If you're bleeding money on software rent, this is the most important 4 minutes of your week."
6. **"Watch to the end because..."** — promise a specific reveal, then pay it off.
7. **Contrarian/enemy** — "Everything you've been told about [X] is why you're stuck."
8. **Receipt hook** — one vivid number/screenshot immediately: "Here's last Tuesday: 11 booked estimates."
9. **Four-part consulting hook** — name segment → acknowledge acute pain → present outcome → future-pace ("by the end you'll know...").
10. **Presupposition** — "Now that you've decided to stop renting your stack..."

Anti-pattern: never open with a name, greeting, or "thanks for watching."

## 7. Mechanism & enemy

- A VSL doesn't sell benefits; it sells **a new understanding of the problem**. The shift: "trust me it works" → "now I understand why it works."
- Two mechanisms, both named with proper nouns:
  - **Problem mechanism (the enemy):** the hidden reason everything they tried failed. It absolves the viewer — "it's not your fault, it's X." Enemy = a system, industry, tool, or practice. Never the viewer.
  - **Solution mechanism (the system):** your named way of attacking that root cause. The product is merely the delivery vehicle for it.
- Sequence: agitate → reveal the hidden enemy → show why past attempts/competitors don't touch it → name your system → product as vehicle.
- House examples: enemy = the digital landlord / the growth tax / the tech-rent racket (Allodra); enemy = the speed-to-lead + follow-up gap (Sierra-6, "it's not your prices, it's not your work"). Systems = allodial title / the 3 Laws of Landed Jobs.

## 8. Application-funnel CTA beats

- Funnel shape: VSL page → apply button → short application (goals, situation, timeline) → calendar → thank-you page that pre-frames the call.
- **Takeaway beat:** "this isn't for everyone" — who it's for AND who it's not for. The filter raises value and repels tire-kickers.
- **Qualification frame:** "apply to see if you qualify"; the call is a diagnosis/strategy session, not a pitch.
- **Pre-frame the click:** say exactly what happens next. Next-step-specific CTAs outperform generic by ~28%.
- **Three-part offer rhythm** before the ask: what we'll do with you → the result to expect → why acting is risk-free.
- Scarcity must be real: builds-per-month caps, cohort close, founding-member counts. No timers.
- Repeat the CTA: soft mid-video (catches early leavers), hard at close; name the button's location literally.

## 9. Proof without testimonials

Sequencing when proof is thin: **demonstration → math → mechanism data → founder story → guarantee.**
1. **Demonstration / screen-share** — show the thing working live.
2. **Receipts** — dashboards, invoices, booking counts; the specificity IS the proof.
3. **Math proof** — cost of the problem per month vs cost of the fix, on screen (the analyzer/calculator is this, productized).
4. **Mechanism-as-proof** — third-party research validating the mechanism, not the product (e.g. the Oldroyd/HBR speed-to-lead study).
5. **Founder scar tissue** — your own before/after as the first case study; stories ≈ 22× more memorable than facts.
6. **Borrowed credibility** — platforms you build on, credentials, media (⚠️ verify every media claim before it ships — see guardrails).
7. **Guarantee as proof** — where a guarantee is allowed (check the brand guardrail; Allodra never offers money-back).
8. **Customer-language proof** — script the problem beats verbatim from replies/calls; converts ~22% higher than invented language.

## 10. The 12 failure modes

1. Slow/self-intro open — loses ~80% before any value lands. The #1 killer.
2. Offer as the hook — the hook is a recognition test ("that's me"), not a compressed pitch.
3. Price before the value stack.
4. Agitation past 90 seconds — reads as manipulation, especially to analytical B2B viewers.
5. Hype crash — infomercial energy up front, timid CTA at the end; tonal dissonance = distrust.
6. No named mechanism — benefits without a "why everything else failed" collapse at the solution beat.
7. Vague or buried CTA — hinting instead of instructing.
8. More than 5 objections handled — signals desperation. Handle 3–5: price, timing, "will it work for me," complexity.
9. Fake scarcity on an application funnel — destroys the qualification frame.
10. Rambling without a script — length without structure is the universal failure.
11. One script for all awareness levels — cold needs problem-first education; warm tolerates offer-early.
12. Buy-now urgency language above an application form — CTA must match the funnel.
